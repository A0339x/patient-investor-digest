Information, not instruction.

This digest exists to keep you informed -- not to prompt you to act. Most weeks, the right move is no move at all. A market that's choppy, rallying, or selling off does not oblige you to change your ranges. Reading is enough.

The sole purpose of data is to inform behavior. Before you open an issue, decide why you're reading. Two good reasons:

  • To learn and discuss. Building your mental model -- how big news in the markets impacts how much fees you're earning, what happens to your range when the market moves depending on if it's wide or narrow, how skewed ranges behave in a trend break and how to make them work in your favor to get more appreciation than your normal LP. This is what Mastermind discussions sharpen, and it compounds into better decisions later.
  • To decide. A position is on the line and this week's news genuinely changes your thesis. The test: would you act any differently after seeing the data?

If the honest answer is neither -- you're checking for a hit of novelty, to see what's moving, to fill idle minutes -- the reading isn't harmless entertainment. It's a dopamine loop that creates emotional damage with no upside, training you to overreact to every wiggle in the market.

The math bears it out. A portfolio returning 15% a year with normal volatility looks positive about 93% of the time when you check it once a year, ~54% of the time when you check it once a month, and barely 50% of the time when you check it once a day. The more often you check, the more the noise swamps the signal. You start seeing losses where there is only variance -- and loss aversion does the rest, making each bad print hurt more than each good print helps. Rebalancing out of emotion instead of thesis is how LPs underperform a simple hold.

So read for understanding, not for action. If a week's news genuinely shifts your thesis on a pair, adjust. Otherwise, the best move is usually the one you don't make.
